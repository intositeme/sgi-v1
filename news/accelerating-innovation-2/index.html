Accelerating Innovation

4 generations of Start-up acceleration reveal untold secrets and how much acceleration is still capable of.

This is part 2 of a 2-part series written by Alex Lin, Head of Infocomm Investments Pte Ltd on the accelerator boom in Singapore and where were off to next.

I talked about the evolution of the Singapore start-up ecosystem from Generations 1 thru to 4 inPart 1 of this series. The Singapore start-up ecosystem is now at the tipping point of Gen 4 into Gen 5, where in a nutshell, accelerators would pivot to beinginnovation enablers, and our entire ecosystem continues to accelerate in tandem.

Lets first talk about whats on the minds of most are accelerators still important in the ecosystem?

Yes, definitely. Acceleration remains greatly beneficial for start-ups. They dont only focus on getting start-ups to close funding rounds, even though many measure success by that metric; intricately associated to being able to raise funds is the start-ups ability to get their product-market-fit (PMF) right during the 3-months boot-camp. When start-ups achieve their PMF, they stand a better chance at achieving business sustainability and viability, which then lends them in good stead to raise (even more) money. And this part the close of funding rounds is what the man on the street reads and hears about.

But what is less talked about is the accelerators ability to wean out start-ups that dont make the cut. With the try fast, fail fast mindset, acceleratees who dont manage to raise funding within 6 months of graduation or see traction often see their businesses fade and wind down eventually. Entrepreneurs can and should move on to their next venture; otherwise they risk being cannibalized by faster moving start-ups from the same cohort. I see this as a healthy recycling of entrepreneurial talent and spirit within an ecosystem.

What is even lesser known or less appreciated is the effect of acceleration on the entire ecosystem that one can learn how to build great start-ups by attending demo-days. The presentations always follow the same format and sequence: Identifiedcustomers; validated customersproblem;impactof problem; followed by how myteamsolves it (better than the others.) Would-be founders and younger start-ups can be subliminally influenced to always think about having better product-market-fit.

While were on the topic of cycles, if you observe, theres a cyclical pattern of concentration and distribution from Generations 1 to 4. Taking the analogy of computing, we started off with mainframe computers (concentrated) then to PC clients/servers (distributed) and now were moving to cloud servers (concentrated). Looking through similar lenses, Gen 1 (business plan competitions) was the start of wide-spread knowledge and activity build-up (distributed). Gens 2 and 3 saw the collective base of knowledge and experience in building start-ups deployed at incubators and commercial accelerators (concentrated). Arriving us at Gen 4, where we are now, were starting to see a knowledge-sharing economy of acceleration methodology (distributed).

Pivoting into Generation 5

So as our ecosystem matures, acceleration know-how spreads and can become institutionalised. There is less need for pure-play accelerators. The accelerator has arrived at the stage of disrupting itself and now needs to be re-invented to becoming aninnovation enabler.And just as how pioneer accelerator JFDI is evolving with the ecosystem, their next steps, next ventures are likely to fit into a Gen 5 with characteristics that include:

  • More & more (and eventually the majority) of the ecosystem stakeholders possess knowledge/know-how of building start-ups (a decentralisation of knowledge);
  • There is the desire to build innovation-driven enterprises (IDEs), which leads to better jobs in the economy (a typical government objective), good markets & revenues (start-ups and corporates both want these), and return on investments (clearly relevant for VCs and Universities);
  • Stakeholders cooperate, seeking complementary alliances and resources, whilst still mindful of staying competitive and of their (own) strategic objectives;
  • Theinnovation enableris involved at the various life stages of a start-up from the beginning through to its growth stages; an end-to-end innovation enabler:Entrepreneur/talent identification and development >> Pre-acceleration >> Acceleration >> Incubation >> Series A and beyond
  • An interconnected web of ecosystems around the world.

All ecosystems eventually get to a stage where innovation is critical to the country. Because innovation gets us ahead of current times, it boosts the economy, creates jobs etc. Even though ecosystems around the world are unique, they are all connected; and this larger ecosystem of innovation and start-ups has the potential to benefit everyone.

While each ecosystem has its own development path based on its resources and constrains, they are all connected by the brain flow, brain share of entrepreneurs who themselves are very connected and are able to tap on each other and each ecosystems strengths to develop their start-ups. Some examples to demonstrate interconnected ecosystems:

  • Israel is a proven source of good tech-based early stage start-ups, and many of these start-ups have and will grow into the US.They seek markets, funding and exits. Over time, talent and networks settle in and enhances the ecosystem.
  • India has a good many start-ups that are not just targeting local needs & situations but have products/services also applicable elsewhere. These start-ups would end up going overseas, to the region, often times via Singapore, with a readier market to support continued expansion.
  • China (Beijing) has its own vibrant start-up ecosystem which is dominated by a few big players (winner takes all in an intensely competitive environment).While the China market is still big, many (next tier) start-ups often seek ideas, technology and business partners from other ecosystems to create additional paths for growth and survival.

"Health of start-ups

I'm often asked how are Singapore start-ups doing/performing ever since the Singapore start-up ecosystem was energized 2 years ago. What they're really thinking about is the start-ups' exit plans more than anything. To the eyes and knowledge of most (largely because of what the media reports), Singapore start-ups don't seem to show for many exits. But CB Insights reported that Singapore is in fact ranked 12th globally when it comes to exits recorded by start-ups. Within the broader ASEAN (Association of Southeast Nations), 40% of exits in Southeast Asia hail from our tiny red dot.

The reason why we don't hear of such exits is because most of these exits fall in the range of $40 - 200 million, deemed too small to be newsworthy by mainstream media. So it's no wonder the man on the street thinks little of Singapore start-ups when it comes to exits.

 

I had earlier shared my thoughts with Business Times when I was drafting this series and theyve concisely summed up the generations that Singapore (as an ecosystem) has been through in the image above. As we transit into the next generation of our ecosystem, I envision all 5 of the stakeholders (the entrepreneurs, risk capital, corporates, government, and universities/research institutions) in our ecosystem model to grow and scale start-ups, helping them arrive at their fullest potential, transforming the respective industries they impact and eventually the society and our world. It is important to note that, the cycle is getting shorter; it took Gens 1 and 2 over 10 years to develop, it took Gens 3 and 4 no more than 3 years.

To excel in Gens 4 and 5, the ecosystem needs to adopt a collaborative attitude toward innovation. Collaborate to Innovate. With this mindset and attitude, and tapping on brain flow and brain share, we can collectively create better worlds and smarter nations around us.
